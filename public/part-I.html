Part I

About Visual Perception

In this part, we isolate three discrete stages of the human perceptual process, and model each stage in a pair of simple computing devices that communicate solely via the visual sense. At each of three stations — Sensing, Interpreting, Reacting — an audience member will view a simple conversation that illustrates one of the isolated stages, and can interact with the conversation by adjusting parameters and disrupting the light between the devices.

1. Sensing

The first pair of devices focuses on Sensing. Each device acts as a mirror, attempting to output on its display exactly what it has received as input to its camera.

Due to a variety of factors — distinct locations of the devices, visual noise in the environment, small aberrations in hardware, the time between input/output, etc. — the reflection is less than perfect, creating an evolving feedback loop. Visitors can adjust the sensitivity parameter to affect how much light the cameras let in, and can experiment with obstructing the space between the devices to alter the feedback loop (quite a pleasing experience!). This pair calls to attention concepts of subjectivity, individuality, and perspective.

In human perception there are many processes involved in enhancing the signal, such as a selective attention to the relevant features of the stimulus or filtering noise. We modeled these processes and use them as filtering before the image is displayed back:

Two devices are looking at and reacting two each other. This pair is inspired by two strategies we use to react to what we see: logic and prediction. We use logic to make decisions based on what we sense. For example, we stop our car if the car ahead of us stops. Prediction is used to anticipate situations in the future, allowing us to time our reactions. To do this, we analyze the behavior of our environment and we compare it wiht similar behavioral patterns we have experienced in the past to make informed decisions.
